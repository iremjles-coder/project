Diseases of the Respiratory System

Parts Diseases
Nose Rhinitis: This is the inflammation and swelling of the mucous membrane inside the nose. It is generally categorized into allergic rhinitis (triggered by allergens like dust mites, pet dander, or pollen) and non-allergic rhinitis (triggered by environmental factors like smoke, chemical fumes, or weather changes). Symptoms include chronic sneezing, a runny nose, and nasal congestion.
Nasal Polyps: These are soft, painless, non-cancerous growths that develop on the lining of your nasal passages or sinuses. They often hang down like tiny grapes or teardrops. If they grow large enough, they can block nasal passages, cause chronic congestion, and severely reduce your sense of smell.
Pharynx (Throat) Pharyngitis: This is the medical term for a sore throat. It is an inflammation of the pharynx, most commonly caused by viral infections or bacterial infections like Strep throat. It causes pain and difficulty swallowing.
Pharyngeal Cancer: A type of throat cancer where malignant cells develop in the tissues of the pharynx, strongly linked to tobacco use, heavy alcohol consumption, and HPV.
Larynx (Voice Box) Laryngitis: An inflammation or infection of the larynx and vocal cords, usually caused by overuse, irritation, or a viral infection, leading to hoarseness or temporary voice loss..
Laryngeal Cancer: Cancer that forms in the tissues of the larynx, with the earliest sign usually being a persistent change in the voice or chronic hoarseness.
Epiglottis Epiglottitis: A severe, potentially life-threatening medical emergency where the epiglottis becomes highly inflamed and swollen due to an infection, which can completely block the airway.
Trachea (Windpipe) Tracheitis: A severe bacterial infection of the trachea, most commonly caused by the bacterium Staphylococcus aureus. Because the trachea is a major airway, the resulting swelling and buildup of thick mucus can cause a characteristic barking cough, high-pitched breathing noises, and dangerous airway obstruction, especially in young children.
Tracheal Stenosis: This is the abnormal narrowing or constriction of the lumen of the trachea. It is most frequently caused by scar tissue formation following prolonged medical intubation (being on a breathing tube in the hospital) or from physical trauma. It restricts the volume of air that can reach the lungs, causing shortness of breath and wheezing.
Bronchi Bronchitis: The inflammation of the lining of the bronchial tubes, which carry air to and from your lungs. It can be acute (short-term, usually developing from a viral cold or flu) or chronic (a long-term, progressive condition characterized by a productive cough that lasts for months, most commonly caused by cigarette smoking).
Bronchospasm: A sudden, involuntary contraction or tightening of the smooth muscles in the walls of the bronchi. This spasm causes the airways to sharply narrow, severely restricting airflow. It is a hallmark feature of asthma attacks and allergic reactions, resulting in sudden wheezing, chest tightness, and difficulty breathing.
Lungs Pneumonia: An inflammatory infection that causes the microscopic air sacs in one or both lungs to fill up with fluid or pus. This makes it difficult for oxygen to transfer into the bloodstream, leading to symptoms like a cough with thick phlegm, high fever, shaking chills, and sharp chest pain during deep breaths.
COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease): A progressive, chronic lung disease that causes long-term breathing difficulties by restricting airflow. It typically involves two co-existing conditions: emphysema, where the walls of the alveoli are progressively destroyed. Chronic bronchitis is constant airway inflammation. Long-term tobacco smoking is the leading cause.
Lung Cancer: A malignant tumor that begins when abnormal cells in the lungs grow out of control, usually originating in the cells lining the bronchi or alveoli. As the tumor grows, it destroys healthy lung tissue, interferes with respiratory function, and can spread to other parts of the body.
Diaphragm Diaphragmatic Hernia: A structural defect or hole in the diaphragm muscle that allows abdominal organs (such as the stomach, intestines, or liver) to shift upward into the chest cavity. This crowding compresses the lungs and prevents them from expanding fully. It can be a birth defect or caused by severe blunt trauma (like a car accident).
Diaphragm Paralysis: The loss of voluntary and involuntary control over the diaphragm muscle, typically caused by damage to the phrenic nerve (the nerve that signals the diaphragm to contract). It can affect one side (unilateral) or both sides (bilateral). When paralyzed, the muscle cannot pull downward to draw air in, causing severe shortness of breath, especially when lying flat.
Pleura (Lung Lining) Pleurisy: An inflammation of the pleura where the layers rub against each other like sandpaper, causing sharp, stabbing chest pain when breathing or coughing.
Pleural Effusion: A condition where an abnormal, excessive amount of fluid builds up in the pleural space, compressing the lung and causing shortness of breath.
Pneumothorax: Commonly known as a collapsed lung, this happens when air leaks into the pleural space and puts pressure on the outside of the lung, causing it to deflate.
